Tier 1 · how models actually work

Gradient descent

The single mechanism behind all model training. You're blindfolded on a hill; you can't see the map, but you can feel the slope under your feet. Step downhill, feel again, repeat. That's the whole idea — now drag the ball and watch it happen.

position x = – loss (how wrong) = – slope = – steps = 0
Drag the ball anywhere on the curve, then press Run.

Try this (in order)

  1. Leave the hill smooth. Set the learning rate very low (~0.1) and Run. Watch it crawl — tiny steps, forever.
  2. Now set it near 0.9 and Reset → Run. It snaps to the bottom in a few steps. That's a good step size.
  3. Crank it past 2.0 and Run. It overshoots and explodes outward — the step is so big it leaps past the valley and climbs the far wall. This is why a wrong learning rate breaks training.
  4. Switch to the bumpy hill. Drag the ball to different starting spots and Run. It lands in different valleys depending on where it started — because it only ever feels the slope right under it, never the whole map.

What you just felt, in words

Loss = the height of the hill = how wrong the model currently is. Training wants to get to the bottom (least wrong).

The slope (gradient) = which way is downhill, and how steep. We can measure it just by checking "is it higher a tiny step to the left or the right?" — which is literally what the code here does.

The step = move a little in the downhill direction. The learning rate is how big that little step is. Too small → crawls. Too big → overshoots and diverges. This single knob is one of the most important in all of ML.

The twist for alignment-adjacent intuition: on the bumpy hill the ball gets stuck in local valleys — it can't see a deeper valley one hill over, because it only feels local slope. Real loss landscapes have billions of dimensions, and "where you end up depends on where you started and how you stepped" is a deep, recurring theme.

Here the x-axis is one knob and the hill is 2-D so you can see it. A real model has billions of knobs (weights), and gradient descent nudges all of them at once, a tiny step at a time, a few trillion steps total — until "predict the next word" falls out the bottom. That is all training is.
